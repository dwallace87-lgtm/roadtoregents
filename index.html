11th Grade U.S. History • Northside Charter High School • Road to the Regents

Road to the Regents: U.S. History Review & Skills Bootcamp

Focus
Regents Exam in U.S. History & Government (Framework) – Daily practice with stimulus based multiple choice, Short Essay Sets 1 and 2, and the Civic Literacy Essay while reviewing content from Reconstruction to modern civil rights.
Timeframe
This plan covers 45 minute classes from early December through mid January. Below are Days 1–21 of the bootcamp, running from December 3–January 14.

Unit Overview – Road to the Regents

This Regents prep sequence is my Road to the Regents plan for my 11th graders at Northside. Most of my students are not yet comfortable 11th grade readers and writers, so the bootcamp is built around:
  • Short, clear mini lessons that chunk content from Reconstruction through the modern civil rights movement.
  • Partner work and talk time every day so students are not reading or writing alone for long stretches.
  • Daily Regents practice – at least a few stimulus based multiple choice questions and a quick written task linked to Short Essay Set 1, Set 2, or the Civic Literacy Essay.
  • Guided note templates based on the event organizers you shared (Context, Significance, Constitutional or Civic Issues, Efforts) so students can turn content into usable essay evidence.
I keep my teacher voice warm and direct. I tell students exactly why each move matters for the Regents: “We are doing this question the same way you will see it on test day, so practice the moves now while we are together.”

Daily Plans – Workshop Structure (Days 1–21)

Day 1 (Dec 3) Launch Road to the Regents & Baseline MC Check
Day focus: Introduce the U.S. History Regents exam structure, lower stress, and get a baseline multiple choice snapshot using content students already saw from the American Revolution through Reconstruction.
Mini Lesson
  • Project a simple slide titled “Road to the Regents” with the exam parts: Part I – MC, Part II – Short Essays (Set 1 and Set 2), Part III – Civic Literacy Essay.
  • Very quickly explain what each part asks students to do and how many points it is worth, then highlight that small improvements on MC and short essays can move a student to passing.
  • On the board, model a “Regents MC Attack Steps” anchor:
    1. Read the source or map title.
    2. Underline key words in the question.
    3. Eliminate two wrong answers first.
    4. Choose best remaining answer and circle it.
  • Emphasize that we will use these same steps every day so they feel automatic by January.
Guided Practice
  • As a class, walk through 3–4 released Regents MC questions from early content (Revolution, Constitution, Civil War) that use short text or a simple map.
  • Teacher thinks aloud with the anchor chart: “The question says ‘a major cause of the American Revolution’. I see ‘taxation without representation’ in choice 2. That matches what we talked about, so I am going to cross off the choices about slavery and women because they do not match this question.”
  • Students answer on whiteboards or scrap paper, then hold up responses so the teacher can see patterns.
  • Quickly celebrate correct answers and correct one common error with clear language rather than shame.
Independent Practice
  • Students complete a 6 question baseline MC mini quiz focused on content up through Reconstruction.
  • Questions use short source snippets so reading load is manageable. Timing is about 10 minutes.
  • Students bubble answers on a very simple half sheet to mimic Regents answer sheets.
  • Teacher circulates to quietly redirect off task students and note who needs reading support for stems and answer choices.
Share / Closure
  • Collect quizzes for quick data. Show an anonymous sample of neat bubbling and label it as a positive example.
  • Ask students to do a one sentence exit ticket in their notebook: “One step that helped me on MC today was...”
  • Preview Day 2: “Tomorrow we dive back into Reconstruction and the Freedmen’s Bureau, and we will start using the event notes pages you will keep for your Civic Literacy Essay.”
Materials / Resources
  • Slides with Regents exam overview and “MC Attack Steps”.
  • 4 released US History Regents MC questions from early content (printed or projected).
  • 6 question baseline MC mini quiz (Revolution to Reconstruction).
  • Student notebooks, scrap paper or mini whiteboards.
Day 2 (Dec 4) Reconstruction & Freedmen’s Bureau – Event Organizer
Day focus: Refresh key Reconstruction ideas and teach students how to use the event note organizer (Context, Significance, Constitutional or Civic Issue, Efforts) for Reconstruction and the Establishment of the Freedmen’s Bureau.
Mini Lesson
  • Quick Do Now: 3 Regents MC questions on Reconstruction (13th–15th Amendments, sharecropping, Black Codes). Students answer independently in notebooks using the MC Attack Steps chart.
  • Check answers as a class with raised hands. Ask students to explain why one wrong answer is wrong to build test language.
  • Project the “Event: Reconstruction” organizer. Walk through the four boxes: Context, Significance, Constitutional or Civic Issues, Efforts.
  • Model filling one bullet in each box with students:
    • Context: “When? Right after the Civil War, starting 1865.”
    • Significance: “Short term: freedmen can vote in some Southern states.”
Guided Practice
  • Students work in pairs with a short, leveled reading on Reconstruction (Radical Republicans, 13th–15th Amendments, military districts).
  • Partners highlight details that belong in the Context and Significance boxes of the Reconstruction organizer.
  • Teacher pauses to think aloud about one paragraph and show how to pull out a short phrase rather than copy entire sentences.
  • Class briefly shares out context bullets and the teacher adds them to a projected version of the organizer.
Independent Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Establishment of the Freedmen’s Bureau” organizer and show the engraving of the Freedmen’s school.
  • Students silently annotate the image using a quick frame: “I see... I think... This shows...”
  • Each student writes at least one bullet in each of these boxes on their Freedmen’s Bureau page:
    • Context: who created it and when.
    • Significance: short term effects for freed people.
    • Issue: example, “lack of education and support for former enslaved people.”
  • Teacher circulates, especially to SPED and MLL students, and offers sentence stems in the issue box like: “A civic issue was that freed people did not have...”
Share / Closure
  • Ask pairs to share one strong Significance bullet about the Freedmen’s Bureau while the teacher collects them on chart paper labeled “Evidence we can use later”.
  • Exit ticket: students answer a short written Regents style question: “Identify one way Reconstruction tried to expand rights for African Americans.”
  • Preview: “Tomorrow we are going to see how some white Southerners and courts fought back against these changes.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 Regents MC questions on Reconstruction (printed or projected).
  • “Event: Reconstruction” organizer and “Event: Establishment of the Freedmen’s Bureau” organizer.
  • Leveled Reconstruction reading (Radical Republicans, amendments, Black Codes, sharecropping).
  • Freedmen’s Bureau school engraving image.
  • Chart paper to record class “significance” bullets.
Day 3 (Dec 5) Backlash to Reconstruction & Plessy v. Ferguson
Day focus: Build a clear picture of backlash to Reconstruction and Jim Crow segregation using the Backlash Against Reconstruction and Plessy v. Ferguson event organizers, and connect these to Regents style questions.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions on Reconstruction backlash and Jim Crow (ex: poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses).
  • Review answers quickly and highlight one vocabulary word students must know for Regents (for example “segregation” or “Jim Crow laws”).
  • Project the Thomas Nast cartoon “The Union As It Was” and give students 30 seconds to silently observe.
  • Ask three quick prompts: “Who do you see? How do you think they feel? What do you notice about the African American figure?”
  • Introduce the idea of “backlash” as resistance to change that tries to pull rights back.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Backlash Against Reconstruction” organizer.
  • In pairs, students read a short paragraph on the end of Reconstruction (Compromise of 1877) and the rise of white supremacist groups and Jim Crow laws.
  • As a class, fill the Context box together on the projected copy. Call on students to add key details: “federal troops leave the South in 1877, white Democrats take back state governments.”
  • Partners then work to draft bullets in Significance and Issue boxes with sentence stems: “A short term effect was...” “A civic issue was that African Americans...”
Independent Practice
  • Give students the “Event: Plessy v. Ferguson” organizer and a brief summary of the case in student friendly language.
  • Students underline the ruling phrase “separate but equal” and circle the amendment number that is being twisted (14th Amendment equal protection).
  • Each student fills:
    • Context: year, what Plessy did, what the Supreme Court decided.
    • Significance: one short term and one long term effect of the ruling.
    • Issue: equal protection under the 14th Amendment.
  • Students then answer a short Regents style constructed response in their notebook: “Explain how Plessy v. Ferguson helped legalize segregation.”
Share / Closure
  • Ask for 2 volunteer answers to the Plessy question and underline phrases that show clear cause and effect (for example “separate but equal let states keep Jim Crow”).
  • Exit ticket: students rate on a quick scale in their notes: “How confident do I feel explaining what Jim Crow was?” (1 low, 3 medium, 5 high).
  • Preview: “Next class we jump into the Gilded Age and the rise of big business and keep building these event pages.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on Jim Crow and Reconstruction backlash.
  • “Event: Backlash Against Reconstruction” organizer.
  • “Event: Plessy v. Ferguson” organizer.
  • Thomas Nast cartoon image “The Union As It Was”.
  • Short reading on Compromise of 1877 and the rise of Jim Crow.
Day 4 (Dec 8) Rise of Monopolies – Gilded Age Economy
Day focus: Introduce Gilded Age industrialization and the rise of monopolies using the Rise of Monopolies event organizer and practice reading political cartoons for Regents style questions.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 2 MC questions on industrialization (for example, railroads, steel, oil) plus 1 MC on the idea of a monopoly or trust.
  • Show the Puck cartoon “Next!”, where trusts are lined up as giant figures.
  • Ask students to quietly write three labels in notebooks: “Big Business,” “Workers,” “Government.”
  • Think pair share: “Which group in this cartoon looks powerful? Which group looks small or weak?”
  • Define monopoly and trust in simple language and connect to Regents vocabulary.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Rise of Monopolies” organizer.
  • Read a short guided note paragraph on Carnegie, Rockefeller, and the idea of “robber barons” vs “captains of industry”.
  • As a class, fill the Context box: “late 1800s, rapid industrial growth, few government rules on big business.”
  • In pairs, students pick two specific examples from the paragraph to add to the Significance box, such as: “higher output and cheaper steel” or “low wages and dangerous working conditions.”
Independent Practice
  • Students answer a short Regents style constructed response tied to the cartoon: “According to the cartoon, how did monopolies affect competition or small businesses?”
  • They must use a detail from the cartoon in their answer (for example, size of the characters, facial expressions).
  • Students then complete the Issue box on their organizer: “A civic issue was that big trusts had too much power and hurt competition.”
  • Teacher collects a few notebooks to spot check if students can convert visuals into written evidence.
Share / Closure
  • Ask 2–3 students to share one bullet from their Issue box and circle similar ideas on the board.
  • Closure prompt on the board: “Why would some Americans start to demand that the government do something about monopolies?” Students answer with one sentence in notebooks.
  • Preview: “Tomorrow we look at government regulation of business and practice a short essay using these two event pages together.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on industrialization and monopolies.
  • “Event: Rise of Monopolies” organizer.
  • Puck cartoon “Next!” image.
  • Short guided note reading on Carnegie, Rockefeller, trusts, and workers.
Day 5 (Dec 9) Government Regulation of Business & Short Essay Set 1 Practice
Day focus: Use the Government Regulation of Business event organizer to understand Progressive trust busting and model a Short Essay Set 1 style response that connects monopolies to reforms.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions that pair a short quote or cartoon with a question about Sherman Antitrust Act and Theodore Roosevelt as a trust buster.
  • Review answers and briefly define: regulation and trust busting.
  • Show the cartoon of Theodore Roosevelt controlling trusts and ask: “What is he doing? Does he look scared of big business or in charge of it?”
  • Explain that Regents loves questions that link Gilded Age problems to Progressive Era solutions.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Government Regulation of Business” organizer.
  • Read a short passage on Sherman Antitrust Act, Northern Securities Case, and Teddy Roosevelt.
  • As a class, complete the Context and part of the Significance boxes: “When? Early 1900s. Why? To limit the power of giant trusts and protect competition and consumers.”
  • Students in pairs list at least two Efforts in the bottom box: “Using Sherman Antitrust Act to break up trusts”, “Creating the Interstate Commerce Commission to regulate railroads.”
Independent Practice
  • Project a Short Essay Set 1 style question, something like: “Explain one way the federal government responded to the problems created by big business in the late 1800s and early 1900s.”
  • Model a quick outline with students using their Rise of Monopolies and Government Regulation of Business event pages as evidence.
  • Students then write a 3–5 sentence short essay response in their notebooks, using the frame: “One problem caused by big business was...” “The government responded by...” “This mattered because...”
  • Teacher collects 4–5 responses to quickly score for use of specific evidence and clear explanation.
Share / Closure
  • Call on one or two students to share part of their answer while you highlight the sentence that best shows cause and effect.
  • Closure reflection in notebook: “How did your event organizers make writing the short essay easier?”
  • Preview: “Next up we shift to Immigration and Urbanization in the Gilded Age and reactions to that, using the same event page strategy.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on Sherman Antitrust Act, ICC, and Teddy Roosevelt.
  • “Event: Government Regulation of Business” organizer.
  • Gilded Age and Progressive regulation reading (short, leveled paragraphs).
  • TR trust busting cartoon image.
  • Short Essay Set 1 style prompt and simple scoring checklist.
Day 6 (Dec 10) Immigration & Urbanization – Reactions to the Gilded Age
Day focus: Use the Immigration and Urbanization in the Gilded Age and Reactions Against the Gilded Age event organizers to connect new immigration, city growth, and labor conflict, while continuing daily MC practice.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions using a short chart or map of immigration patterns (for example, Ellis Island arrivals) and a tenement photo.
  • Review answers and define or review: urbanization, tenement, nativism.
  • Project a tenement photograph and ask students to list three words it makes them think of (crowded, dark, unsafe, etc.). Record responses on the board.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Immigration and Urbanization in the Gilded Age” organizer.
  • Read a short paragraph out loud that describes push and pull factors and basic city conditions.
  • Class helps fill the Context box together: “late 1800s to early 1900s, many immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe, cities grow fast, lots of factory jobs.”
  • In pairs, students highlight two details from the reading that belong in the Significance box and write them in their own words.
Independent Practice
  • Give students the “Event: Reactions Against the Gilded Age” organizer and a copy of a labor vs monopoly cartoon such as “The tournament of today”.
  • Students first annotate the cartoon in pairs: “Where do you see labor? Where do you see big business?”
  • Individually, they complete:
    • Context: strikes, labor unions like Knights of Labor, early populist farmers.
    • Issue: unsafe working conditions, low wages, child labor.
  • Students then answer one Regents style MC question that uses the cartoon, and one short constructed response: “Identify one way workers responded to Gilded Age conditions.”
Share / Closure
  • Ask volunteers to share one Effort they listed in the Reactions organizer (for example, forming unions, going on strike, supporting the Populist Party).
  • Closure prompt: “If you had to write a short essay about problems in the Gilded Age, which three event pages would you pull as evidence?” Students circle three organizers in their notebook and jot a one word reminder of the issue next to each.
  • Preview: “Next we will move into Progressive Era reforms and keep connecting event pages to short essay writing and later to the Civic Literacy Essay.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on immigration and urbanization (chart, map, photo based).
  • “Event: Immigration and Urbanization in the Gilded Age” organizer.
  • “Event: Reactions Against the Gilded Age” organizer.
  • Tenement photo and labor vs monopoly cartoon.
  • Short reading on push and pull factors, city life, unions and Populist reactions.
Day 7 (Dec 11) Progressive Era Reforms – Fixing Gilded Age Problems
Day focus: Use the Progressive Era Reforms event organizer to see how reformers tried to solve Gilded Age problems and practice a short, Regents style written response that connects cause and effect.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 Regents MC questions that connect Gilded Age issues to Progressive solutions (for example, meatpacking to Pure Food and Drug Act, monopolies to Sherman Antitrust Act, tenements to settlement houses).
  • On the board, draw a quick T chart: “Gilded Age Problems” on the left and “Progressive Solutions” on the right. Ask students to suggest one from each of the last two days (monopolies, unsafe work, child labor, etc.).
  • Introduce the “Event: Progressive Era Reforms” organizer and show the cover of The Jungle by Upton Sinclair.
  • Explain that this event page will give them easy evidence later for questions about government trying to improve life for workers and consumers.
Guided Practice
  • Read a short, leveled excerpt from The Jungle aloud. Students underline one sentence that shows a problem in meatpacking.
  • As a class, fill the Context box on the Progressive organizer: “Early 1900s, journalists called muckrakers exposed problems in cities, factories, and politics.”
  • In pairs, students highlight key reforms in a short reading (Pure Food and Drug Act, Meat Inspection Act, direct election of senators, initiative and referendum, women’s suffrage).
  • Partners add at least two bullets to the Significance box: “Government passed laws to protect consumers”, “People gained more voice in government.”
Independent Practice
  • Students complete the Issue and Efforts boxes on the Progressive organizer using sentence stems: “A civic issue Progressives tried to fix was...” and “One effort by government or reformers was...”
  • Then they answer a Short Essay Set 1 style prompt in their notebook: “Describe one Progressive Era reform and explain how it attempted to solve a problem from the Gilded Age.”
  • Students are required to name the reform and connect it directly to a specific problem (for example, unsafe food, corrupt political machines, child labor).
Share / Closure
  • Ask two volunteers to read one strong sentence from their short response that clearly shows cause and effect.
  • Closure reflection: “Circle which event pages we have now for this era: Monopolies, Regulation, Immigration and Urbanization, Reactions, Progressivism. Which one feels easiest to use as evidence for you, and why?”
  • Preview: “Tomorrow we turn from problems at home to American Imperialism and expansion overseas.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 Regents MC questions on Progressive Era reforms.
  • “Event: Progressive Era Reforms” organizer.
  • Short excerpt from The Jungle, plus a summary paragraph on Progressive reforms.
  • T chart template or board space for “Problems vs Solutions”.
Day 8 (Dec 12) American Imperialism – “Ten Thousand Miles from Tip to Tip”
Day focus: Use the American Imperialism event organizer to build a clear picture of why the United States expanded overseas and practice MC questions that use political cartoons and short text.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 Regents MC questions on Spanish American War and imperialism (causes, results, and debate over annexing the Philippines).
  • Project the cartoon “Ten Thousand Miles from Tip to Tip”. Ask students: “What is Uncle Sam holding? What lands can you name from the labels?”
  • On the board, list three motives for imperialism based on quick class brainstorm: economic, military, cultural.
  • Explain that they will use this cartoon and a short reading to fill the American Imperialism event page as future essay evidence.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: American Imperialism” organizer.
  • Read a short summary aloud about Spanish American War, annexation of Puerto Rico, Guam, the Philippines, and debates over empire.
  • As a class, complete the Context box: “late 1800s, USA defeats Spain, gains overseas territories, wants new markets and naval bases.”
  • In pairs, students add bullets to the Significance box describing short term and long term effects such as: “United States becomes a world power”, “debates over whether imperialism matches American ideals.”
Independent Practice
  • Students complete the Issue box: “A constitutional or civic issue was whether the Constitution followed the flag and if people in new territories had full rights.”
  • They then answer one MC question using the cartoon and one short constructed response question: “According to the cartoon, what new role is the United States taking in the world?”
  • Students must mention at least one territory from the cartoon and explain what that suggests about US power.
Share / Closure
  • Ask volunteers to read one Issue bullet and underline key words on the board that show why this might come up on the Civic Literacy Essay later (for example, rights, expansion, power).
  • Quick reflection: “Do you think imperialism was more about money, the military, or culture? Circle one in your notes and write one reason.”
  • Preview: “Next class we zoom in on the Roosevelt Corollary and how it changed the Monroe Doctrine.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 Regents MC questions on Spanish American War and imperialism.
  • “Event: American Imperialism” organizer.
  • “Ten Thousand Miles from Tip to Tip” cartoon.
  • Short summary of American imperialism (Philippines, Puerto Rico, Guam, Cuba, debates at home).
Day 9 (Dec 15) Roosevelt Corollary – Big Stick in the Caribbean
Day focus: Use the Roosevelt Corollary event organizer to understand how the United States expanded its role in Latin America and practice a short essay that compares American Imperialism and the Roosevelt Corollary.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions on Monroe Doctrine and Roosevelt Corollary, using a simple map of the Western Hemisphere.
  • Project the cartoon “Theodore Roosevelt and his Big Stick in the Caribbean” and ask: “What is he doing? How big is his stick compared to the other countries?”
  • On the board, write: “Monroe Doctrine: keep Europe out” and “Roosevelt Corollary: US can step in” to set up the difference.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Roosevelt Corollary” organizer.
  • Read a short summary as a class about US intervention in the Caribbean and Latin America, including examples like Dominican Republic and Panama Canal.
  • Together, fill the Context box: “Early 1900s, US worried about European powers collecting debts in Latin America, so Roosevelt adds the Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine.”
  • Students in pairs add bullets to the Significance box that describe effects on Latin America and on US power.
Independent Practice
  • Students complete the Issue and Efforts boxes: “A civic issue was US intervention in other countries’ affairs”, “Efforts included using the military and controlling finances in some Latin American nations.”
  • Then they write a short essay style answer connecting imperialism and the Roosevelt Corollary: “Explain how the Roosevelt Corollary extended American imperialism in Latin America.”
  • Students should mention at least one example (for example, Panama Canal, Dominican Republic) and link it to US power.
Share / Closure
  • Invite one student to read their best sentence that shows the connection between American Imperialism and the Roosevelt Corollary.
  • Closure: “In one word, write how Latin American countries might have felt about this policy.” Students write the word in their notebook and, if comfortable, share with a partner.
  • Preview: “Tomorrow we move into World War I and how the United States gets pulled into a global conflict.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on Monroe Doctrine and Roosevelt Corollary.
  • “Event: Roosevelt Corollary” organizer.
  • Big Stick cartoon image.
  • Short summary reading on US interventions in Latin America.
Day 10 (Dec 16) World War I – Causes and U.S. Entry
Day focus: Use the World War I event organizer to review the causes of WWI and reasons for U.S. entry and drill MC questions that use maps and short texts.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions that use a map of alliances in 1914 and a short quote about unrestricted submarine warfare.
  • On the board, write the M A I N causes: Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism.
  • Ask students to match each word with a simple symbol or drawing in their notebook to help them remember.
  • Explain that Regents questions love asking why the US entered the war in 1917 and how that changed US foreign policy.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: World War I” organizer.
  • Read a short summary out loud of the causes of WWI, US neutrality, Lusitania, Zimmerman Telegram, and US entry.
  • As a class, fill the Context box: “1914 war begins in Europe, 1917 US joins Allies after German submarine attacks and Zimmerman Telegram.”
  • In pairs, students add two bullets to Significance, such as: “US helps Allies win, becomes more involved in world affairs.”
Independent Practice
  • Students complete the Issue box with a focus on civil liberties or neutrality vs involvement: “A civic issue was how much the US should get involved in foreign wars and how the government limited rights during wartime.”
  • Students answer one MC question about a war bonds poster and write a short response: “Identify one reason the United States entered World War I.”
  • Teacher circulates and quickly corrects vague responses by pushing students to name a specific event or reason.
Share / Closure
  • Have students turn and talk to a partner: “What is one detail you added to your WWI event page that you did not remember before today?”
  • Closure: “In one sentence, explain why the US could no longer stay neutral.” Students write this in their notes.
  • Preview: “Next class we look at Effects of WWI inside the US including the Red Scare, women’s roles, and the Great Migration.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions using WWI alliance map and war posters.
  • “Event: World War I” organizer.
  • Short summary of causes, US neutrality, and US entry into WWI.
  • Simple M A I N visual for student notebooks.
Day 11 (Dec 17) Effects of World War I in the United States
Day focus: Use the Effects of World War I in the United States event organizer to track changes on the home front and practice a Short Essay Set 2 style response using multiple events.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions on the Sedition Act, Great Migration, and 18th/19th Amendments.
  • Project a War Savings Stamps poster and ask: “What is this poster asking Americans to do?” “How does this show total war?”
  • Explain that Regents often ask about how war changes life inside the US, not just battles overseas.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Effects of WWI in the United States” organizer.
  • Read a short passage describing war bonds, women in factories, the Great Migration, and the Red Scare.
  • Work as a class to fill the Context box: “During and after WWI, the US government and society changed to support the war and adjust to peace.”
  • In pairs, students highlight one social effect and one political effect and place them in the Significance box.
Independent Practice
  • Students complete the Issue box with a focus on civil liberties and fear of radicals: “A civic issue was how much the government could limit free speech and target immigrants during the Red Scare.”
  • Then students write a Short Essay Set 2 style response that uses two events: “Identify two effects of World War I on the United States and explain each effect.”
  • Encourage them to pull from the WWI event page and the Effects event page (for example, Great Migration and women’s suffrage).
Share / Closure
  • Ask one or two students to share their two effect choices, while you list them on the board as a quick class brainstorm for future essays.
  • Closure: “Star one effect that you think Regents is most likely to ask about and write one reason.”
  • Preview: “Next class we step into the Roaring Twenties and culture clashes of the 1920s.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on home front WWI changes.
  • “Event: Effects of WWI in the United States” organizer.
  • War Savings Stamps poster image.
  • Short reading on the Great Migration, Red Scare, women’s role, and Prohibition.
Day 12 (Dec 18) Roaring Twenties & Social Movements of the 1920s
Day focus: Use the Roaring Twenties and Social Movements of the 1920s event organizers to understand economic boom and culture clashes and practice MC and short response questions with ads, photos, and posters.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions on 1920s topics such as flappers, Prohibition, and the automobile.
  • Project the Life magazine cover from 1926 and ask: “What do you see that shows fun, leisure, or modern life?”
  • On the board, create a quick T chart: “Boom” vs “Backlash” to set up prosperity and social conflict.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Roaring Twenties” organizer.
  • Read a short summary about consumer goods, credit, jazz, and new roles for women.
  • As a class, fill the Context box: “After WWI, US experienced economic growth, new technology, and cultural change in the 1920s.”
  • In pairs, students highlight boom details (cars, radios, movies, jazz, flappers) and add them to the Significance box.
Independent Practice
  • Give students the “Event: Social Movements of the 1920s” organizer and a Temperance poster image.
  • Students annotate the poster: “What is the problem? What is the solution the poster supports?”
  • Students complete the Context and Issue boxes for Social Movements, including Prohibition, religious fundamentalism vs science, and nativism.
  • Short constructed response: “Identify one way the Roaring Twenties was a time of conflict between old and new values.”
Share / Closure
  • Students share one example of “boom” and one example of “backlash” from their organizers with a partner.
  • Closure prompt: “If you got a Regents short essay about the 1920s, which event page would you use first and why?” Students write one sentence in their notebook.
  • Preview: “Next class we build out the Harlem Renaissance page and keep adding to our 1920s evidence bank.”
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on Roaring Twenties and Prohibition.
  • “Event: Roaring Twenties” organizer.
  • “Event: Social Movements of the 1920s” organizer.
  • Life magazine cover image, Temperance poster image.
  • Short reading on 1920s culture, Prohibition, and culture wars.
Day 13 (Dec 19) Harlem Renaissance – Art, Pride, and Protest
Day focus: Use the Harlem Renaissance event organizer to understand how Black art and culture showed pride and protest, and practice a short response students can reuse on the Civic Literacy Essay.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 Regents style MC questions on the Harlem Renaissance (Great Migration, writers, jazz, culture in Harlem).
  • Project the image “Couple in Raccoon Coats, 1932” and ask: “What do you notice about their clothing, car, and faces? What story does this tell?”
  • On the board, list key words that students share: pride, style, luxury, success, city life.
  • Explain that the Harlem Renaissance is both cultural pride and a quiet form of protest against stereotypes and racism.
Guided Practice
  • Hand out the “Event: Harlem Renaissance” organizer.
  • Read a short, adapted paragraph about the Great Migration, Harlem as a Black cultural center, and figures like Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, and Duke Ellington.

Day 21 (Jan 14) Civic Literacy Essay Boot Camp – Courts, Rights, and Students
Day focus: Use the Warren Court and Supreme Court Rulings that Affect Students and Schools event organizers plus earlier notes to plan and write a full Civic Literacy Essay using at least two events or cases.
Mini Lesson
  • Do Now: 3 MC questions on major Warren Court decisions (Brown v Board, Gideon v Wainwright, Miranda v Arizona, Tinker v Des Moines).
  • On the board, write the basic pieces of the Civic Literacy Essay: Issue or Question, Background, Events or Cases with Evidence, Impact and Evaluation.
  • Explain that today they will practice writing one full Civic Literacy Essay using event pages as their evidence bank.
  • Quickly review how to turn organizer boxes into sentences: Context = background, Significance = effects, Issue and Efforts = actions and results.
Guided Practice - Warren Court and Student Rights
  • Hand out the “Event: The Warren Court” and “Event: Supreme Court Rulings that Affect Students and Schools” organizers.
  • Read short summaries describing how the Warren Court expanded rights of the accused, equal protection, and freedom of speech; and a quick overview of Tinker v Des Moines and other student cases.
  • As a class, fill the Context box for Warren Court: “In the 1950s and 1960s the Supreme Court under Chief Justice Earl Warren made decisions that expanded civil rights and civil liberties.”
  • Students in pairs complete key bullets in the Significance boxes for both event pages, focusing on how court rulings affected daily life and student rights.
  • Build a quick planning chart on the board: Possible Issues (equal protection, fair trials, free speech in school) and list matching events or cases under each one.
Independent Practice - Full Civic Literacy Essay
  • Give students a simplified Civic Literacy prompt, for example: “Choose two Supreme Court decisions or rights movements that expanded rights in the United States. Explain the issue, describe actions taken, and evaluate the impact of each example.”
  • Students choose from: Brown v Board, Warren Court criminal rights cases, Tinker v Des Moines, African American Civil Rights Movement, Modern Civil Rights Movements, or another event page they feel confident about.
  • They quickly complete a two column planning chart in their notebooks: Example 1 and Example 2 with spaces for Issue, Actions, and Impact, using their organizers as support.
  • Students then spend the bulk of the period drafting the Civic Literacy Essay introduction and two body sections. Strong writers can add a short conclusion that compares the impact of the two examples.
  • MLL and SPED supports: sentence frames for introduction and body paragraphs, option to dictate to a scribe, and chunked goals (finish intro first, then one body paragraph, then the second).
Share / Closure
  • Quick partner share: each student reads their thesis sentence and one strong evidence sentence to a partner. Partners check: “Did they name the example?” “Did they explain impact?”
  • Whole class debrief: “Which event or case felt easiest to write about and why?” Take two or three responses and add those events to a visible list titled “Go To Civic Literacy Examples”.
  • Final reflection in notebooks: “One thing I can do on my own to get ready for the Regents is...” Students write one realistic next step such as reviewing a specific event page or practicing MC questions.
  • Remind students where their organizers and essays will be kept so they can review them closer to exam day.
Materials / Resources
  • 3 MC questions on Warren Court and student rights.
  • “Event: The Warren Court” organizer.
  • “Event: Supreme Court Rulings that Affect Students and Schools” organizer.
  • Civic Literacy Essay prompt and simple planning chart template.
  • Sentence frames and checklists for introduction, body paragraphs, and impact explanation.
